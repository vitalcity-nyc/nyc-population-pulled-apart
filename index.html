New York City's population, pulled apart

For over a decade, New York City has been caught in a tug-of-war: residents leaving for other parts of the country, immigrants arriving from abroad, and a slowly shrinking natural advantage of births over deaths. New Census estimates released today show how an immigration crackdown brought the city's growth engine to a halt -- and into reverse.

The pandemic cratered the city. Immigration rebuilt it. Now that's gone.

NYC lost over 300,000 people in a single year during COVID. A historic wave of international migration -- asylum seekers, visa backlogs clearing -- brought it most of the way back. Then in 2025, the immigration crackdown cut that flow by 70%, and the city is shrinking again.

The migration seesaw

NYC has always lost people to other parts of the U.S. and gained them from abroad. When international migration is strong enough to offset domestic losses, the city grows. When it isn't, the city shrinks. In 2025, it wasn't.

Sources and methodology

Data sources: U.S. Census Bureau, Vintage 2025 county-level Population Estimates (CO-EST2025-ALLDATA) and Vintage 2020 county-level estimates (CO-EST2020-ALLDATA) for NYC borough data. State-level data from NST-EST2025-ALLDATA, NST-EST2020-ALLDATA, and annual components tables (2003-2009 series).

Definitions: NYC totals are the sum of the five boroughs (Bronx, Kings/Brooklyn, New York/Manhattan, Queens, and Richmond/Staten Island counties). "Natural change" = births minus deaths. "International migration" = net movement from abroad. "Domestic migration" = net movement to/from other U.S. counties. All population estimates are as of July 1.

Notes: The 2020-2025 and 2011-2020 data use different Census Bureau vintages. Small residual adjustments (not shown) account for differences between the sum of components and total change. The 2020 row in the Vintage 2025 series covers a partial year and overlaps with the Vintage 2020 series; we use each vintage's full-year data and avoid double-counting.

Limitations: Census population estimates are modeled from administrative records (IRS data, Medicare, immigration files), not direct counts. International migration estimates were substantially revised upward in recent vintages. Domestic migration is derived from IRS and other address-change records and may undercount certain populations.
