New York City's population, pulled apart

For over a decade, New York City has been caught in a tug-of-war: residents leaving for other parts of the country, immigrants arriving from abroad, and a slowly shrinking natural advantage of births over deaths. New Census estimates released in March 2026 show how a drop in international migration brought the city's growth to a halt -- and into reverse.

A COVID crash, an immigration rebound, and a sharp reversal

NYC lost over 300,000 people in a single year during COVID. A wave of international migration -- asylum seekers, visa backlogs clearing -- brought it most of the way back. Then in 2025, international arrivals fell by 70%, and the city lost population again.

Three forces, one city

Every year, NYC's population is shaped by three competing forces. Domestic outflow (red) has been consistently negative. International inflow (blue) has varied sharply from year to year. And natural change (green) has been slowly shrinking.

The migration seesaw

NYC has always lost people to other parts of the U.S. and gained them from abroad. When international migration is strong enough to offset domestic losses, the city grows. When it isn't, the city shrinks. In 2025, it wasn't.

Sources and methodology

Data sources: This project follows the approach used by the NYC Department of City Planning, Population Division: intercensal estimates for completed decades, and the latest postcensal vintage for the current decade.

Population figures: 2011-2019 population values use the Census Bureau's 2010-2020 Intercensal Estimates, which distribute the gap between postcensal estimates and the 2020 Census count across the decade. 2020-2025 population values use Vintage 2025 postcensal estimates. State-level population follows the same approach, with 2003-2009 from the 2000-2010 Intercensal Estimates.

Components of change (births, deaths, migration): The Census Bureau does not publish intercensal components at the county level. Following DCP's practice, components for 2011-2020 come from Vintage 2020 (CO-EST2020-ALLDATA) and components for 2021-2025 come from Vintage 2025 (CO-EST2025-ALLDATA). Components may not sum to the population change shown, because the population and component figures come from different census series.

Why this matters: Different census vintages can produce substantially different estimates for the same year. Each new vintage revises the entire series with updated methodology and data. Using a single postcensal vintage for a completed decade ignores corrections made possible by the subsequent census. Intercensal estimates are the Census Bureau's most accurate mid-decade figures because they incorporate both the opening and closing census counts.

Definitions: NYC totals are the sum of the five boroughs (Bronx, Kings/Brooklyn, New York/Manhattan, Queens, and Richmond/Staten Island counties). "Natural change" = births minus deaths. "International migration" = net movement from abroad. "Domestic migration" = net movement to/from other U.S. counties. All population estimates are as of July 1.

Limitations: Census population estimates are modeled from administrative records (IRS data, Medicare, immigration files), not direct counts. International migration estimates were substantially revised upward in recent vintages. Domestic migration is derived from IRS and other address-change records and may undercount certain populations.
